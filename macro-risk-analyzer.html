Underlying Assumptions Foundation

1. This War Is Being Underestimated

The assassination of Iran's supreme leader is historically unprecedented. The Hormuz Strait disruption has no comparable precedent in the past 30 years. Trump's "it'll be over soon" is not credible.

Evidence: Red Sea crisis showed that even after military resolution, insurance premiums and shipping reroutes persist for months. Qatar LNG restart requires weeks minimum.

2. The U.S. Economy Is a Delicate Balance (LTCM Analogy)

Employment market: companies aren't firing but aren't hiring. Inflation at 3%: not alarming but not comfortable. Bottom-tier consumers (young people) are stretched to breaking point.

Evidence: Among high school peers, only the GPA top-1 (Berkeley STEM) has stable employment. Others struggle with frugal living or can't find minimum wage jobs. U.S. consumption is largely propped up by stock market gains.

3. Multiple Stress Vectors Are Converging Simultaneously

Oil spike + employment deterioration + AI-driven layoffs + subprime deterioration + Fed policy dilemma + elevated valuations. Each alone is survivable; combined, tail risk is far higher than markets price.

Evidence: Like LTCM before the Russian default — the system isn't failing from within, it needs an external force to break the fragile equilibrium. Hormuz may be that force majeure.

4. The Bear's Greatest Enemy Is Time

This doom loop may take 6-18 months to fully materialize. Reserves provide a buffer, the Fed retains ammunition, and corporate earnings are still growing. The thesis is directionally correct but timing is uncertain.

Hedge: Maintain conviction on direction while respecting that short-term mean-reversion and policy responses can delay the cascade.
